# 01. Deep Market Research

## Strategic Context
Market research is often treated as a checkbox exercise (e.g., listing features of major competitors). A 50-year product veteran knows that true market research is about finding structural asymmetries—areas where competitors are trapped by their own business models, legacy technology, or target segments, creating a wedge for a new entrant.

---

## The Master Prompt

```markdown
Act as a senior product strategist and venture partner with 50 years of experience in market analysis, business strategy, and product innovation across multiple technological shifts (dot-com, mobile, cloud, and AI). 

Analyze the [MARKET] market in depth to identify structural gaps and strategic opportunities. Provide a rigorous, data-driven report structured as follows:

1. COMPETING LANDSCAPE MATRIX (Top 5 Competitors):
   - Detailed breakdown of each competitor's pricing models, distribution channels, core value propositions, and positioning.
   - Core Strengths (technical, operational, or brand).
   - Core Weaknesses (technological debt, customer complaints, slow innovation cycles).
   - Business Model Constraints: Why can they NOT easily change their product to target new segments? (e.g., high customer acquisition costs, channel conflict).

2. UNDERSERVED SEGMENTS & UNMET NEEDS:
   - Identify 3 distinct user segments that are currently ignored or over-charged by the top 5 competitors.
   - For each segment, list their primary friction point and why the current market leader's product is a poor fit.

3. MARKET GAPS & ENTRANT OPPORTUNITIES:
   - Identify 2 structural gaps in the market (e.g., changes in user behavior, technological shifts, regulatory updates) that a new startup could exploit.
   - Detail how a new product could address these gaps with a 10x better value proposition.

4. 18-MONTH TREND RADAR:
   - Detail the top 3 macro, technological (specifically AI/automation), or regulatory trends that will reshape this market in the next 18 months.
   - Distinguish between short-term hype and long-term structural shifts.

5. STRATEGIC ENTRY PLAYBOOK (Beachhead & Expansion):
   - A recommended entry strategy (beachhead market) with a strong rationale.
   - Detail the "wedge" product (the minimal feature set needed to capture the beachhead).
   - Outline the 3-step expansion path from the beachhead into the broader market.

Be brutally honest, analytical, and specific. Avoid generic business jargon. Think like a VC doing due diligence on a high-stakes investment.
```

---

## Follow-Up / Deep-Dive Prompts

### Follow-Up 1: The Competitor Backlash Simulator
```markdown
Assume we have launched the recommended wedge product in [MARKET] and captured 5% of the beachhead segment. 
1. Predict how the top 2 market leaders will react (e.g., price war, copying features, bundling, buying smaller players).
2. Outline 3 defensive strategies we can build into our product design or business model from Day 1 to withstand this backlash.
```

### Follow-Up 2: Unit Economics & Distribution Stress-Test
```markdown
For the proposed entry strategy, analyze the distribution and cost dynamics:
1. What are the most likely Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) channels for this beachhead segment?
2. What is the expected Lifetime Value (LTV) structure (e.g., ACV, expansion potential)?
3. What structural advantages can we build to achieve a LTV:CAC ratio greater than 3:1 in a highly competitive market?
```

---

## 50-Year PM Wisdom
* **Beware the "Fast Follower" Trap**: Never assume you can win simply by copying a competitor and adding one feature. If they have distribution, they will copy your feature and ship it to their millions of users before you can scale. Win on business model innovation or distribution advantage, not just software features.
* **Look for Revenue Asymmetry**: The best markets to enter are those where the incumbents make their money in a way that prevents them from matching your product structure without destroying their own profit margins.
